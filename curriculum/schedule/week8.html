Is optimization even possible?

If you're about to set out on a quest, it helps to know whether your quest will be successful! The  Extreme Value Theorem states that

If a function \(f\) is continuous on the closed interval \([a,b]\), then there are \(c\) and \(d\) in \([a,b]\) so that for all \(x\) in \([a,b]\), \(f(c) \leq f(x)\) and \(f(x) \leq f(d)\).
You can read more on  Page 138 of the Textbook. The upshot is that continuous functions on closed intervals attain their maximum and minimum value, so, yes, in many cases our search for the “optimum” value is not a fool's errand!

Okay, so if optimization is possible, how do I do it?

There's a process for  finding the maximum and minimum values of a function \(f\) on a given domain. You should

  • differentiate \(f\),
  • find critical points,
  • check critical points and endpoints, and
  • check the behavior near the left and right hand endpoints, if you're working over, say, an open interval.

Key points are to  remember to check the endpoints and  remember that critical points include points of nondifferentiability.

You can  practice finding maximums and minimums on mooculus.

Why would I want to optimize a function?

In the real world, we must makes choices, and wouldn't it be great if we could make the best choice? For instance, you might  want to enclose the most area inside a rectangular fence. In broad strokes, the process goes as follows.

  • Draw a picture;
  • write down your goal—what quantity you are trying to maximize or minimize;
  • note any constraints;
  • rewrite your goal as a function of a single variable;
  • apply calculus—use what we've learned to find the value which maximizes or minimizes your function of a single variable.

You can read more about this on  Page 141 of the Textbook.

It is worth pointing out that  you do not necessarily need calculus to solve many of these problems since you can use, for instance, the AM-GM inequality. And although problems like  finding the optimal shape of a soup can are a ton of fun, there are certainly other considerations to worry about in the real world which are likely more important than surface area.

Nevertheless, other examples like  where three bubbles meet are rich mathematically, and can be seen in the real world. If you want to consider  how large of an object can be taken around a corner, you should print it out and try it for yourself.
