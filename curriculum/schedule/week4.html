How do I differentiate a product?

To differentiate the product of two differentiable functions, use  the product rule, which states that

$$\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx} \left( f(x) \, g(x) \right) = f'(x) \, g(x) + f(x) \, g'(x).$$

That looks wrong! But  it makes sense geometrically, and  there's a formal proof which you can read on  Page 72 of the Textbook.

Be sure to  practice computing with the product rule.

How do I differentiate a quotient?

Once you can differentiate a product, you might suspect there is a quotient rule. Indeed,  there is a quotient rule but it can be  difficult to remember considering that the quotient rule says that

$$ \displaystyle\frac{d}{dx} \displaystyle\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \displaystyle\frac{f'(x)\, g(x) - f(x)\, g'(x)}{g(x)^2}. $$

You will find a proof of the quotient rule on  Page 75 of the Textbook; another proof will appear next week, by combining the power rule, product rule, and the chain rule.

Be sure to  practice computing with the quotient rule.

Higher derivatives?

Since the derivative is a function, we can differentiate the derivative to get the second derivative of a function. But that raises a question:  what do these higher derivatives mean?. If we consider  the sign of the second derivative, we'll find that it encodes information about the concavity of the function; there are some graphs on  Page 60 of the Textbook, and you can  play with concavity on mooculus.

Once we are talking about derivatives in the abstract, you might wonder  whether $$\frac{d}{dx}$$ has any meaning by itself. You might think of $$\frac{d}{dx}$$ sort of like you think of $$f$$ as opposed to $$f(x)$$, by which I mean $$\frac{d}{dx}$$ is “the derivative not applied yet to anything.” If you keep taking more math classes (and I hope you will!), you'll learn more about this perspective when you study differential operators.

How can I sketch a graph without a computer?

Richard Hamming wrote in the preface to one of his books that “the purpose of computing is insight, not numbers.” I agree! Given a function, your goal is to describe the qualitative features of that function.

Chief among those qualitative features are  extreme values, which you will find defined on  Page 53 of the Textbook. Once we know we're looking for extrema, we have to figure out a way to find what we desire;  Fermat's theorem, which you'll find on  Page 54 of the Textbook helps. It says that the only place you'll find a local minimum or a local maximum is at a critical point, meaning an input $$x$$ where either $$f'(x) = 0$$ or $$f'(x)$$ doesn't exist.

By combining information about extrema and the sign of the first derivative and the sign of the second derivative, you can  sketch graphs which illustrate the qualitative features of a function. The procedure is outlined on  Page 67 of the Textbook.

Sometimes people are tricked into thinking that, if you're standing at a local minimum, the function is decreasing to your left and increasing to your right. There are some  pathological examples which should dissuade you from that notion.

Practicing the  various skills related to curve sketching is a good way to review much of what we've done in the course thus far—write down a random function, and see if you can draw a picture of your random creation!

How do I differentiate $$e^x$$?

Remember back to Week 2, when we used the Intermediate Value Theorem to find  fixed points of functions, meaning inputs $$x$$ so that $$f(x) = x$$? We can ask if there is a fixed point of $$\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}$$, meaning a function which is unchanged when you differentiate it. Admittedly, $$f(x) = 0$$ is such an example, but a more exciting example is  the function $$f(x) = e^x$$, which is also its own derivative. You can read the proof on  Page 50 of the Textbook.
