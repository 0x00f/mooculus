Functions? What's a function?

Functions are the main star of our course. Calculus isn't just about numbers—it's about relationships between things, and understanding how one thing changing affects something else. A “function” keeps track of these relationships.

To understand limits in general, we focus in on  the specific case of \( \displaystyle\lim_{x \to 1} \displaystyle\frac{x^2 - 1}{x-1} \). Trigonometry provides two additional examples, namely

  •  \( \displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \displaystyle\frac{\sin x}{x} \) and
  •  \( \displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \sin \left(\displaystyle\frac{1}{x}\right) \).

To gain deeper understanding, read  Chapter 1 of the Textbook.

To learn how to compute limits, we often employ rules like

  •  the limit of a sum is the sum of the limits,
  •  the limit of a product is the product of the limits,
  •  the limit of a quotient is (almost!) the quotient of the limits.

You can see these worked out in detail in  Section §1.3 of the Textbook.

Why do I care?

Limits are the basic way to figure out, for instance,  how fast a ball is falling at a specific moment in time.
