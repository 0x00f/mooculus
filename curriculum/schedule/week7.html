How do derivatives help with limits?

It is a bit surprising that  derivatives can help us to evaluate limits. This is l'Hôpital's rule, and initially, it is only helpful for limits where the numerator and denominator are heading towards zero, but  it is possible to transform quite a few limit problems so that l'Hôpital's rule applies.

Be sure to  practice using l'Hôpital's rule. You can find l'Hôpital's rule on  Page 112 of the Textbook.

As a bit of a warning: sometimes students get so very excited about l'Hôpital's rule that they want to use it everywhere, but please  do not fall in love with l'Hôpital.

How do derivatives help me understand rates of change in the real world?

On  Page 121 of the Textbook, you'll be reminded that the derivative is, fundamentally, measuring the rate of change. For example, you can use derivatives to  discuss the rate of growth of bacteria, or you can use derivatives to measure  how quickly the distance between you and a passing car is changing; the derivative can then be heard as doppler shift.

How do derivatives help me understand how two rates of changes are related?

If you have an equation relating two quantities, and both those quantities are changing, you can explore the relationships between those rates of change; these are related rates, and you can find an introduction to the subject on  Page 127 of the Textbook.

The basic technique to solving such a problem goes as follows.

  • Draw a picture, labeling the relevant quantities.
  • Write down an equation, using your diagram as a starting point.
  • Differentiate the equation, remembering to regard quantities as depending on a variable \(t\), for time.
  • Evaluate the result to find whatever you wanted to know about.

In practice, it gets tricky, because these are word problems so there are many opportunities for confusion!

A classic question is  how fast a shadow's length changes as you walk, which you can also  attempt on mooculus. Another classic is studying  how fast a ladder slides down a building.

You can see these things in the real world by thinking about  bowls filling with water or  balloons filling with air. Often you will have to use certain geometric facts, like  the volume of a cone in order to make progress.

There are  quite a few related rates problems on mooculus. Remember you can use the forward arrow key to skip past problems, and you can see  all the problems in a list.
